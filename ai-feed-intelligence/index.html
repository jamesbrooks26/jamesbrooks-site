The problem

The truth about LinkedIn scrolling

Most B2B founders scroll LinkedIn every morning with no real system. They like a few posts, leave a vague comment, and move on. They are present without being purposeful.

The problem is not the time they spend. It is what they do with it. Without a structured way to track which ICPs are active and what they are talking about, engagement becomes random. Random engagement produces random results.

This workflow turns your LinkedIn feed into an intelligence system. Each week you know exactly which of your target ICPs are posting, what they are focused on, and the precise angle to use when you engage.

The Feed Blind Spot: most founders treat their LinkedIn feed as a news source rather than a sales intelligence tool.

Every post from an ICP is a data point. Every comment they leave is a signal. Most founders miss all of it.

The shift:

Old way

Scroll passively and engage when something catches your eye. Each week starts from zero.

New way

Run a structured weekly tracker that tells you who to engage with and why. Each week builds on the last.

That single shift turns your LinkedIn time from a habit into a system.

01

The ICP Activity Scanner

Identifies which of your target ICPs are active this week and what signals they are sending

What you're doing: Building a weekly target list of ICPs worth engaging with. Engaging with the wrong posts is wasted time. This prompt ensures every comment this week reaches someone in your target network and arrives at the right moment.

ROLE: B2B sales intelligence analyst. Your job is to surface the highest-value LinkedIn engagement opportunities from a list of target ICPs.

TASK: Using the inputs below, analyse each ICP's recent LinkedIn activity and produce a prioritised weekly engagement list.

INPUTS:
ICP list: [name / role / company for each, 10 to 20 people]
Recent activity observed: [for each ICP, describe any posts, comments, or activity visible on their profile this week]
Your ICP profile: [paste your ICP profile from Resource 01]

OUTPUT a prioritised table with these columns:
- ICP name and role
- Post type or activity observed
- Signal this sends (what it tells you about their mindset or buying stage)
- Priority level: Very High / High / Medium
- Recommended engagement angle (one sentence)

RULES:
- Prioritise ICPs with active pain posts or growth signals above passive content
- Flag anyone who has posted in the last 48 hours as Very High priority
- 10 ICPs maximum per week. Quality over volume.
- Output: a clean table ready to use as your engagement agenda
OUTPUT: A prioritised weekly list of ICPs to engage with, with one engagement angle per person
02

The Engagement Angle Generator

Produces three comment angles per ICP designed to open conversations rather than close them

What you're doing: For each high-priority ICP from Prompt 1, generating three distinct ways to engage with their post. Different frameworks work for different post types. This prompt gives you options and tells you which to use.

ROLE: B2B relationship strategist. Your one metric is comment-to-reply rate.

TASK: Write three engagement angle options for the ICP post below using these frameworks:

Framework 1 – The Informed Question: ask something specific that only someone with relevant experience would ask
Framework 2 – The Contrarian Add: agree with the core point, then introduce a nuance that invites a reply
Framework 3 – The Shared Observation: reference a relevant result or pattern without naming your service

INPUTS:
ICP role and company type: [who they are]
Post topic or excerpt: [paste or describe the post]
Signal this post sends: [from your Prompt 1 output]
Your core expertise: [what you help with, one sentence]

RULES:
- Each angle must be 2 to 3 sentences only
- No emojis, no CTAs, no service mentions
- The goal is a reply from the ICP, not likes from their audience
- Output: three numbered angles with framework label and one-line rationale for when to use each
OUTPUT: Three ready-to-use comment angles per ICP, each designed to open a conversation
03

The Weekly Feed Tracker

Builds a simple running record of engagement so relationships compound over time

What you're doing: Logging each engagement so the system has memory. Without a tracker, every week starts from scratch. With one, your engagement compounds. Run this on Friday. It formats your weekly activity into a record you can act on the following Monday.

ROLE: B2B pipeline strategist. You help founders convert consistent LinkedIn engagement into trackable relationship progress.

TASK: Using the inputs below, create a weekly feed tracker entry for each ICP you engaged with this week.

INPUTS:
ICP name and role: [from previous prompts]
Post they published: [brief description of the post you engaged with]
Comment you left: [paste the comment you used]
Their response (if any): [paste or describe their reply]
Connection status: [not connected / request sent / connected]

RULES:
- Score the engagement quality 1 to 5 (1 = no reply, 5 = active conversation)
- Flag if a connection request is now appropriate
- Recommend the next engagement action for this ICP next week
- Note the cumulative relationship stage: Cold / Warming / Warm / Hot
- Output: a structured tracker entry ready to paste into your weekly log
OUTPUT: A structured weekly tracker entry per ICP showing engagement quality, connection status, and next recommended action
Worked example · Caldwell Strategic Advisors

3 new ICP connections in week one. 7 engagement scores of 4 or above.

A B2B advisory firm targeting founders of €3M to €10M professional services businesses. They had a clear ICP but no system for tracking who was active. Each week looked the same: scroll, like, occasionally comment, repeat.

Here is what the workflow produced in week one.

Prompt 1 output: Weekly Priority List (top 4 of 15 ICPs tracked)

Post typeSignalPriorityEngagement angle
Founder shares revenue milestoneGrowth mindset, scalingHighAsk what changed in their pipeline to hit the number
CEO venting about missed targetsActive pain, open to changeVery HighValidate the frustration, share a brief observation
Hiring post for sales roleScaling signal, budget unlockedHighAsk what is driving the need for headcount right now
Post on referral-based growthProblem matches offerVery HighOffer a nuanced counterpoint without pitching

Prompt 2 output: Engagement Angles for Top ICP

ICP: CEO of €6M professional services firm | Signal: Posted about missing Q3 target and blaming referral slowdown

Angle 1 (Informed Question): "Referral slowdowns hit differently when they are your primary channel. What did Q3 tell you about where the pipeline is actually coming from, and where it is not?"
Angle 2 (Contrarian Add): "Missing a target because of referral dependency is a systems problem, not a sales problem. Most firms try to fix it by adding headcount. The ones who fix it structurally do it differently."
Angle 3 (Shared Observation): "Firms at your stage often find that the referral slowdown is not the real issue. It is the absence of anything running in parallel. The gap shows up in the numbers before anyone wants to name it."

They selected Angle 2. The CEO replied within four hours. A connection request was sent and accepted the same day.

Week one.

3 new ICP connections. 7 engagement scores of 4 or above. Two ICPs moved from Cold to Warming after a single structured comment.

All of it from 15 minutes on Monday morning.

What to do right now
  1. Build your ICP list before you run Prompt 1. Start with 10 to 15 names. These are founders or decision-makers you want in your pipeline.
  2. Run Prompt 1 on Monday morning. Block 15 minutes. This sets your engagement agenda for the week.
  3. Leave your first comments on Monday or Tuesday using Prompt 2. Prioritise ICPs posting about active problems.
  4. Log each engagement using Prompt 3 on Friday. This turns random activity into a compounding system.
  5. Review your tracker the following Monday. ICPs who replied move up the priority list. ICPs who did not get a different angle next week.
